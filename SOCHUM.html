UN Social, Cultural and Humanitarian Council (SOCHUM)

“Peace is a difficult thing to measure. It is a bit like counting the people who did not die and the wars that did not happen. We give such an importance to the word peace but we do not tend to notice it when it occurs, or report on it. Sometimes it takes reminding ourselves of how terrible the war once was to see the peace growing around us.”

In lands ravaged by war, where human lives seem trivial in the light of their want for power, states have no respect for the fundamental human rights. But how important is it that we know what is happening around us, especially when the outcomes of these wars can completely turn our lives upside down. How imperative is this need for knowledge when such information could be in fact fanning the flames of the already raging war.

Right by the side of the journalists who are jeopardizing their lives to give us this knowledge of the war battered regions, lay the silent yet extremely crucial Humanitarian workers who remain to be decisive in the outcome of the casualties of a war and post war construction. In what hazardous conditions do they function, and is it truly right of us to expect them to continue doing so?

Delegates of the Social Humanitarian and Cultural Committee, we leave the answers of these pertinent questions in your hands

AGENDA: Discussing workers’ rights with special reference to journalists and humanitarian workers in war-torn countries
